The Problem

Simulation can improve policies, but it can also break them.

Improving real-world policies on hardware is slow and expensive, so we turn to simulation. But simulation is a double-edged sword. Optimize freely and you discover reward-hacking behaviors that don’t transfer to hardware; over-constrain to the demonstrations and you inherit their slow, imprecise failures. SCORE takes the middle path: improve within the support of the real-world prior, surfacing better behaviors that are already reachable without inventing ones that aren’t.

The key insight

Policy improvement in simulation should be constrained to the support of the real-world prior.

Method

Steer through the support, don’t escape it.

Beyond the Benchmark

SCORE handles more than the eight tasks.

Continuous operation

Picking cubes and dropping them into a basket, over and over, tests robustness across a long horizon. The base policy is slow and imprecise, leaving the basket mostly empty; SCORE reliably grasps the cube and fills it.

Fast to iterate on new tasks

SCORE is fast: in under half a day, from collecting demonstrations to deployment, we can iterate on a new task. In both below, the base policy is imprecise and can’t recover from a mistake; SCORE is more precise and retries at the right moments.

Why You Shouldn’t Optimize Freely in Simulation

It looks great in sim, then breaks on hardware.

Optimizing freely with unconstrained RL, the policy maximizes reward by exploiting the simulator: contorted, high-force grasps that score well in sim but are erratic or dangerous on the real robot. The Distributional Constraint Tradeoff

Distributional constraints introduce a tradeoff between improvement and transferability.

A natural way to keep simulation policies deployable is to regularize them toward the base policy with a behavior-cloning (BC) loss. But the strength of that constraint is a delicate knob: too little and the policy drifts into dangerous, non-transferable behavior; too much and it inherits the base policy’s failures. No single setting recovers SCORE’s performance.

The same tension on hardware

On real tasks too, distributionally-constrained policies still settle on behaviors that are unsafe or unreliable once deployed.

Steering to a new object: bottle → carrot

We take a frozen bottle-grasp policy and steer it in simulation onto a carrot, an object it never trained on. A carrot is much thinner and demands a precise pinch, a behavior that appears only rarely under the bottle prior. Steering still finds it: on hardware the carrot-steered policy reaches 67%, far above the same policy steered to a bottle (22%).

Where it runs thin

The same bottle policy was trained without distractors. Steering it to handle one recovers a working grasp on hardware (56%, up from 0% base), but it succeeds mainly when the bottle sits on one side of the workspace: steering an out-of-distribution policy to succeed everywhere in simulation is much harder.

The lever

It all comes down to the behavior prior. The broader the prior, the further steering can push.
